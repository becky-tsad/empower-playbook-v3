Building Your Team

The right people in the right roles — trained, supported, and empowered to solve problems — are what separate a poverty exit system from another well-meaning program.

Executives Program Managers 10 min read

The model doesn't run itself. The right people in the right roles — trained, supported, and empowered to solve problems — are what separate a poverty exit system from another well-meaning program.

The Core Question

How do you build a team that can actually move families out of poverty — not just manage them through it? By hiring for problem-solving instinct over credentials, investing in leadership infrastructure, and treating navigator recruitment and retention as the binding constraint on your entire model.

8.1

The Four Core Roles

Every Empower implementation needs these four roles operational before enrolling families.

Role Responsibility Non-Negotiable?
Executive Director Vision, external relationships, funding strategy, policy advocacy. Owns the model's sustainability and growth. Yes
Program Director Day-to-day operations, navigator supervision, partner coordination. Ensures the methodology is followed. Yes
Project Coordinator Data management, reporting, dashboard maintenance. Turns raw data into actionable intelligence for navigators and leadership. Yes
Lead Navigator Senior navigator who mentors other navigators, handles complex cases, and serves as the bridge between ground-level work and program leadership. Yes

Supporting Roles (Strongly Recommended)

Role Responsibility When to Add
Grant Writer / Researcher Funding sustainability — pipeline development, proposal writing, funder relationship management From Phase 1 if possible. Dedicated capacity recommended.
Employer Liaison Certification program, job pipeline development, employer relationship management Phase 1 or early Phase 2
Partner Coordinator Agency relationships, systems coordination, convening facilitation When partner network exceeds what the Program Director can manage alone
Additional Navigators Direct participant support, caseload coverage Scale based on enrollment — maintain manageable caseloads

Scaling the Team

Implementation Size Minimum Staff Navigator Ratio
Small pilot (1-3 counties, 50 families) 4 core roles + 1-2 navigators Manageable caseloads per navigator
Medium (5-8 counties, 125 families) 4 core roles + 4-6 navigators + employer liaison Same ratio maintained
Full (14 counties, 250+ families) 4 core roles + 10-17 navigators + full supporting team EUC operates with 17 navigators across 14 counties
8.2

Hiring for Fit, Not Just Skills

The difference between a poverty management program and a poverty exit system often comes down to the people running it. Skills can be taught. Mindset cannot.

What to Screen For

Problem solvers, not checkbox people. The Empower Method encounters barriers that don't fit in a procedure manual. A family's childcare falls through the week they start a new job. An employer's HR policy conflicts with a benefits cliff transition plan. A housing waitlist jumps from 6 months to 18 months. The navigator who succeeds is the one who finds a solution, not the one who documents the problem and moves to the next case.

The Interview Question That Matters

"Tell me about a time you solved a problem that wasn't in your job description."

Cultural fit indicators:

  • Mission-driven — they're here because they believe families can exit poverty, not just be managed through it
  • Solutions-oriented — their instinct is to fix, not to escalate
  • Comfortable with ambiguity — they can work effectively when the playbook doesn't cover the situation
  • Relationship-builders — navigators succeed through trust, which requires genuine human connection
  • Embedded in the community — navigators who know the landscape of their counties outperform those who don't

What to Screen Out

  • Compliance-first mindset — navigators who prioritize paperwork over people will replicate the system families are trying to escape
  • Referral-only orientation — "I connected them to the right agency" is not the same as "I made sure the agency actually helped them"
  • Burnout risk without support — this work is emotionally demanding. Hire people who can sustain it, and then give them the support to do so
8.3

The Leadership Gap

Most programs are understaffed at the leadership level. This creates bottlenecks, burnout, and decision paralysis.

The pattern: An organization hires navigators (ground-level capacity) but doesn't invest in the Program Director, Project Coordinator, or employer liaison roles that make navigator work effective. Navigators end up doing systems coordination, data entry, employer outreach, and their own supervision. The model collapses into overworked case management.

The Rule of Thumb

You need enough leaders to actually move things, not just report on them. If your Program Director is spending 80% of their time on data reporting, they're not directing the program. If your Executive Director is managing navigators, they're not building funding sustainability.

The three-team connection: The three-team architecture (Chapter 5) exists partly to solve this. The Economic Stability Team handles families. The Collective Impact Team handles systems coordination. The Leadership Team handles policy and funding. When all three functions land on two people, nothing gets done well.

Ongoing Training

Training is not a one-time event. The model evolves. Staff turn over. Community conditions change.

  • Return to the shared vision quarterly — ensure the team is still aligned on goals and methodology
  • Benefits cliff tool updates — as policy changes, cliff points shift. Staff must stay current.
  • Navigator peer learning — regular case conferences where navigators share barrier solutions
  • Employer relationship management — ongoing education as the certified employer network expands
What Kills Programs (Preview of Chapter 9)

Training once and never revisiting. Organizations that treat onboarding as the end of professional development produce checkbox staff within 12 months.

8.5

Navigator Recruitment and Retention

Navigators are the model's most critical human asset — and in rural labor markets, the hardest to recruit.

The Recruitment Challenge

In counties where the top employers are the hospital, the school system, and one manufacturer, the talent pool for navigator roles is thin. You're looking for people who are mission-driven, solutions-oriented, embedded in their communities, and willing to do emotionally demanding work for compensation that competes with healthcare and education salaries.

What Works

Recruit from the community, not from job boards. The best navigators know their counties — the churches, the employers, the informal networks that no service directory captures. They've often worked in social services, healthcare, education, or community organizations. Some are program graduates themselves.

Recommended Approach

Pay competitively. Navigator roles that pay below the local market for comparable positions (social work, community health, case management) will produce constant turnover — creating the exact relationship disruption the model is designed to prevent. If navigators can earn more at the hospital, they will. Budget accordingly.

Build the navigator career pathway. Navigator to Lead Navigator to Program Director is a real career trajectory. Making this visible during recruitment attracts people who want to grow, not just fill a role. The graduate-to-navigator pipeline (Ch 5.5) adds another entry point.

Invest in retention as seriously as recruitment. Navigator burnout is real. The work is emotionally intensive. Manageable caseloads, peer support, regular supervision, and ongoing training aren't perks — they're the infrastructure that keeps navigators effective and employed.

The Scale Constraint

Navigator recruitment is the binding constraint on scaling the Empower Method. You cannot scale faster than you can recruit and train qualified navigators. The Empower Academy training sequence (Ch 14) ensures quality. But the pipeline of people who enter that training depends on compensation, community engagement, and organizational reputation.

For Funders

When an organization requests funding for navigator compensation above minimum levels, that's not overhead inflation. It's the investment that determines whether navigators stay long enough to complete the 18-24 month relationships that produce outcomes. Underpaying navigators is the most expensive mistake an implementation can make.

For Funders and Policymakers

When an organization asks you to fund leadership positions — an Executive Director, a Program Director, a Project Coordinator — that's not overhead. That's the infrastructure that makes navigator work effective. The organizations that underinvest in leadership are the ones that produce the fragmented results you're already tired of funding. The four core roles aren't administrative bloat. They're the architecture of a poverty exit system.

Key Takeaway

Building an effective Empower team requires four non-negotiable core roles before any families enroll, hiring for problem-solving mindset over credentials, investing in leadership infrastructure as seriously as frontline capacity, and treating navigator recruitment and retention as the binding constraint on your model's growth. Skills can be taught. Mindset cannot.

The right people in the right roles — trained, supported, and empowered to solve problems — are what separate a poverty exit system from another well-meaning program.
