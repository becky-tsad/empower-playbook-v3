The Proof

Promises don't move families out of poverty. Results do. Here's what 926 families across 14 rural Tennessee counties prove.

Executives Funders 15 min read

Not projections. Not pilot numbers from a small sample. Verified outcomes from a 4-year, $25 million state-funded initiative across 14 rural Tennessee counties — four years of real-time data collection, third-party validation, and continuous learning.

The Core Question

Does the Empower Method actually work? 926 families, $7.3 million in income gains, and a 4.4:1 return on investment — independently validated — answer that question definitively.

3.1

Empower Upper Cumberland Outcomes

Who These Families Are: Stranded Talent, Not Crisis Cases

These are ALICE families — Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed. 69% came to Empower already working, earning too much for assistance but too little to cover basic needs. They weren't in crisis. They were stuck. Hard work should pay off. The Empower Method makes sure it does.

A Note on Measurement: From FPL to ALICE

The Empower pilot initially used the Federal Poverty Level (FPL) as its benchmark — because that's what existing systems measured. As the model matured, we transitioned to the ALICE threshold (225% of FPL) because it's a far more accurate measure of actual self-sufficiency. FPL says a family of three isn't poor at $24,860. ALICE recognizes that same family needs $54,000+ to cover basic needs. Some data in this chapter references FPL because it was the measurement standard when the data was collected. The target has always been the same: genuine stability, not a federal formula.

Beyond the Numbers: System-Level Wins

The Empower Method doesn't just move families. It moves entire systems. In Upper Cumberland, the model triggered:

  • 800+ childcare slots opened through policy advocacy — The Development District, in partnership with Empower UC, worked with state legislators to lift fire code restrictions that had capped childcare facility capacity. The regulatory change increased allowable children per facility from 25 to 49, creating 800+ new slots across the region and clearing the top barrier standing between families and employment.
  • 49 new childcare jobs — created in Upper Cumberland as facilities expanded to meet new capacity. Solving one poverty-level barrier created livable-wage careers in another sector.
  • $407,159 in household debt reduction through SmartDollar financial coaching — families not just earning more but building wealth.
  • 89% of families actively engaged with navigator support and tools, compared to 40-60% typical participation rates in standard poverty programs.
Research Finding: Two-Generation Works

National research validates the Empower approach. EMPath (urban Boston) achieves 97% employment rates. CAP Tulsa (urban Oklahoma) sees 82% certification rates in healthcare pipelines. Empower UC matches these nationally recognized programs — built for rural communities where no comparable model existed.

Third-Party Validation

We didn't grade our own homework. Two independent research organizations evaluated Empower.

An 11% difference translates to concrete impact: in a cohort of 100 families, 11 additional families permanently exit poverty when using the Empower Method instead of standard services.

Year 2+: Control Group Converts to Empower Model

By Year 2, control group families transitioned into the Empower UC model — because the data proved it worked. Standard services couldn't compete with dedicated navigation, benefits cliff mapping, and coordinated wraparound support.

What this proves: families choose intensive support when they see it works. The model isn't theoretical. It's competitive against the status quo — and families demonstrate that through their engagement.

ROI Analysis: 4-Year Verified Outcomes

Across 926 families over four years, outcomes demonstrate a 4.4:1 return on investment. For every $1 invested in intensive navigation, $7.10 returns through reduced benefit costs, increased tax revenue, and lifetime earnings gains.

This isn't charity math. This is economic infrastructure math. The methodology aligns with approaches used by the Urban Institute and comparable research organizations.

Research Finding: Wraparound Models Work

Research on coordinated, whole-family service models — including studies of community schools and similar wraparound approaches — consistently shows that coordinated services outperform siloed programs. Community schools report returns as high as 15:1. Empower's 4.4:1 ROI, focused on adult economic mobility, follows the same evidence-based pattern.

Research from the Atlanta Federal Reserve on benefits cliff coaching found that 90% of families made concrete action plans after CLIFF coaching sessions — validating that benefits cliff navigation drives behavioral change. When families see their benefits cliff mapped out clearly, they act.

Critical Research Finding: Duration Matters

Empower UC's data shows navigator relationships of less than 6 months produce negative outcomes. Short-term interventions don't just fail — they actively harm families by starting processes families can't complete. They create dependency without building capacity. This is why the Empower Method requires 18-24 month commitments. Anything less is worse than nothing.

What the Evidence Shows — and What It Doesn't

Sophisticated funders and policymakers don't just want to see strong numbers. They want to know the boundaries of those numbers. Here's what we know, what we're still learning, and where the evidence has limits.

The RCT Is Year 1 Only

MEF Associates conducted a randomized comparison during Year 1: some families received Empower's intensive navigation, while a control group received standard DHS services. The results — 11% higher positive outcome rate, $400/month income advantage — are genuine experimental evidence.

By Year 2, control group families transitioned into the Empower model because the outcomes were demonstrably better. This is a powerful behavioral signal — families chose intensive support when given the option. But it also means there is no longer an active control group. Years 2-4 outcomes are pre-post, not experimental.

What This Means for Funders

Year 1 evidence is RCT-level. Years 2-4 evidence is strong observational data from 926 families but does not have an active comparison group. The MEF Associates evaluation is ongoing.

Defining the 92% Employment Rate

92% refers to the employment rate among families enrolled in the Empower program. This measures families with at least one employed adult during program participation.

Important context: 69% of families were already employed at intake. The program's contribution is not primarily moving people from unemployment to employment — it's moving employed families from low-wage, stalled positions to career pathways with wage progression. 125 previously unemployed participants gained employment through the program.

The 4.4:1 ROI: What's Included and What's Not

The 4.4:1 return on investment measures direct program costs against quantifiable economic returns: reduced benefit costs, increased tax revenue, and earnings gains.

What's NOT included in the 4.4:1:

  • System-level impacts: 800+ childcare slots, 49 childcare jobs, policy changes. These generate economic value but are not monetized in the current ROI calculation.
  • Intergenerational effects: Children in stable, higher-income households have measurably better educational and economic outcomes. These are real but occur over decades and are not captured in a 4-year analysis.
  • Employer value: Reduced turnover, workforce stabilization, and talent pipeline benefits to certified employers.
Conservative Estimate

If system-level impacts were monetized, the ROI would be higher. The 4.4:1 is a conservative, defensible floor — not a ceiling.

The $24.3M Projected Lifetime Savings

This figure projects the cumulative taxpayer savings over the working lifetime of families who achieve self-sufficiency. It includes reduced benefit expenditure, increased tax contributions, and avoided social costs.

Assumptions to note:

  • Based on families maintaining self-sufficiency long-term (post-program stability data is still accumulating)
  • Uses standard economic projection methodology aligned with Urban Institute approaches
  • Subject to the same limitations as any lifetime projection — actual outcomes depend on economic conditions, family circumstances, and policy changes

What this means for funders: Use the $24.3M as a directional indicator, not a guaranteed outcome. The annual verified numbers ($7.3M in income gains, $744K in TennCare savings, $183K in payroll tax revenue) are the hardest data points.

What We're Still Learning

  • Long-term retention: Do families who graduate above the ALICE threshold stay there? Post-program follow-up data is accumulating but not yet mature enough for definitive reporting.
  • Job quality over time: Certified employers set a floor ($14.50/hr, benefits, career growth). Tracking actual wage progression and job quality at 12, 24, and 36 months post-placement is an active data priority.
  • Replication fidelity: UCHRA and UCDD's agency adoption is testing whether outcomes hold when the model transfers to different agencies and populations. Early data is collecting.
Why Transparency Strengthens the Case

Programs that claim perfection aren't credible. Programs that show strong results AND acknowledge limitations demonstrate the kind of intellectual honesty that serious funders require. The evidence for the Empower Method is strong, independently validated, and growing. Presenting it accurately — including its boundaries — is more persuasive than overstating it.

Adaptability Proof Points

Built for Partnership: System-Level Integration

The Empower Method was never designed as a standalone program. It's a systems intervention. From day one, UCHRA (Human Resource Agency) and UCDD (Development District) — the backbone agencies of Upper Cumberland — were embedded in design and implementation.

They are now integrating core Empower elements into their own workforce development and career services work with non-TANF populations. What they're testing:

  • Navigator-led support model with workforce development families
  • Benefits cliff mapping for all career services clients, not just TANF-eligible
  • Shared data dashboard to break coordination silos across agencies

The Empower Method wasn't built for one program or one funding stream. UCHRA and UCDD's expansion proves the model works across populations and agencies when the core principles stay intact: dedicated relationships, benefits navigation, and coordinated wraparound support that moves families into livable-wage careers and keeps them there.

Recovery Program Integration

Empower and recovery programs do the same fundamental work — they just meet people at different stages. Recovery focuses on stabilization: sobriety, housing, mental health. But what happens after someone stabilizes? Without a clear path to livable-wage careers, relapse rates climb and families cycle back into crisis.

The Empower Method fills that gap. The navigator doesn't show up just for job placement — they walk alongside the person from stabilization through employment, benefits cliff navigation, and long-term economic mobility. One consistent relationship. When a crisis hits, the navigator is already there, connecting the person to the right support before a setback becomes a relapse.

Key Takeaway

926 families. $7.3M in income gains. 4.4:1 ROI. 83 families at full self-sufficiency. Third-party validated. Control group converted. This isn't a pilot anymore. It's a proven model ready for replication — built for rural communities, validated by independent research, and already expanding beyond its original population.

The evidence is built. What you do with it is the only remaining question.
