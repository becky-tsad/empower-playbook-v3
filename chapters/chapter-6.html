The Certified Employer Program

The missing piece in most workforce programs. Job placement without employer readiness creates revolving doors.

Executives Program Managers 8 min read

Workforce programs spend months preparing participants for employment. Almost none prepare employers for participants. The Certified Employer Program closes that gap.

The Core Question

What happens when you prepare families for jobs but don't prepare employers for families? You get a revolving door — high turnover, frustrated employers, and families cycling back to square one. The Certified Employer Program solves both sides of the equation simultaneously.

Certified Employer Benefits at a Glance
Direct
Access to a trained, job-ready talent pool matched to your open positions
Trained
Staff prepared for specific roles with skills aligned to employer needs
Retention
Post-placement support through an employer liaison keeps employees stable
Benefits
Health coverage and career growth pathways that build employee loyalty
6.1

Why Employer Certification Matters

Job placement is not job retention. Placing a family in a job with an employer who doesn't understand ALICE realities, benefits cliffs, or the support systems their new employee depends on creates a revolving door — high turnover, frustrated employers, and families cycling back to square one.

The Two-Sided Problem

  • Participants need employers who pay livable wages, offer benefits, provide career growth, and understand the transition they're navigating.
  • Employers need reliable workers, reduced turnover, and a talent pipeline they can count on. They're losing employees to the benefits cliff and don't even know it — 1 in 3 businesses report employees turning down promotions because a raise means losing benefits.
The Solution

Employer certification solves both sides. Participants get employers who are ready for them. Employers get workers who are ready — and a support system that continues after placement.

6.2

Certified Employer Requirements

To become a Certified Employer in the Empower network, businesses must meet six standards:

1. Poverty IQ Training — Complete the free training course that covers benefits cliff effects and employee retention strategies. Every certified employer must understand the economic realities their new employees face.

2. ALICE Orientation — Basic education on the ALICE population: who they are, what they're navigating, why traditional HR approaches don't work for this workforce.

3. Benefits Cliff Tool Access — Employers can plan raises, promotions, and hour increases without accidentally triggering catastrophic benefit loss for their employees. This protects the employer's investment in training and onboarding.

Minimum Compensation Standards

4. Compensation Requirements:

  • Entry-level pay: $14.50/hour minimum
  • Positions aligned to participant needs and employer demand (full-time, part-time, or flexible scheduling)
  • Employee benefits package required
  • Career growth opportunities must be available

5. Retention Partnership — Agrees to work with the employer liaison post-placement. When an employee hits a barrier (childcare crisis, transportation breakdown, health issue), the employer communicates with the liaison, who coordinates with the navigator to resolve the issue instead of letting it lead to termination.

6. Wage Subsidy Participation — Certified employers receive 50% wage reimbursement for up to 320 hours through UCHRA. That's approximately eight weeks of full-time wages covered — reducing the employer's risk in hiring participants while the employee establishes their track record.

6.3

The Two-Way Pipeline

Traditional workforce programs push participants toward employers. The Certified Employer Program builds a two-way pipeline:

Employer to Program

  • "I have 5 positions opening in 3 months in healthcare/manufacturing/skilled trades"
  • "Here's what we need: reliable transportation, basic certification, ability to work second shift"

Program to Participant

  • "Let's get you trained and ready for those specific slots"
  • "Here's the certification you need, here's the timeline, here's how your benefits cliff looks at that wage"

Post-Placement to Retention

  • Employer liaison stays engaged with the employer after hiring, coordinating with the navigator on participant needs
  • Employer flags issues to the liaison early instead of terminating
  • Benefits cliff transitions are managed proactively as wages increase
Key Distinction

This isn't job matching. It's career pipeline construction — with the employer liaison as the coordination point between employers and navigators, ensuring the participant, the employer, and the benefits system all move in sync. If a mom needs an evening job on Mondays and Tuesdays, the liaison works with certified employers to find that match. The goal is creating jobs that participants need and opening jobs that employers need filled.

6.4

What Employers Get

Certified employers aren't doing charity. They're making a business decision.

Benefit Details
Trained talent pool Direct access to job-ready candidates trained for specific positions aligned to the employer's actual needs
Reduced turnover Post-placement employer liaison support keeps employees stable through crises that would otherwise cause them to quit
Flexible job matching Positions matched to participant availability and employer demand — full-time, part-time, or flexible scheduling
Health benefits guidance Support navigating employer-sponsored health coverage transitions as employees move off public benefits
Ongoing liaison support Employer liaison stays engaged post-hire, coordinating with navigators — not a "place and forget" model

The employer value proposition in one sentence: You get reliable workers, reduced turnover costs, wage subsidies, and a support system that stays engaged after the hire.

6.5

Building Your Employer Network

Start Small, Build Credibility

  1. Identify 3-5 anchor employers in your region's strongest sectors. In Upper Cumberland, that's healthcare, manufacturing, and skilled trades.
  2. Lead with the business case, not the social mission. Employers respond to turnover reduction, wage subsidies, and talent pipeline access. The mission matters — but the math closes the deal.
  3. Deliver on the first placements. Your first certified employers are your proof of concept. If those placements succeed and retention holds, employer referrals follow.

Expand Through Results

  1. Celebrate certified employers publicly. Recognition matters. Employers who invest in their communities should be visible.
  2. Let employers recruit employers. The strongest pitch for certification comes from a peer who's already done it and seen the results.
  3. Track and share retention data. Employers want numbers. Show them: placement rates, 90-day retention, 1-year retention, turnover cost savings.
The Rural Advantage

In rural communities with 15 significant employers across 14 counties, you can certify every single one. Urban programs can't do this. Rural programs can build employer networks that cover the entire regional labor market — creating a closed loop where every major career pathway runs through a certified, navigator-supported employer.

A Note on Employer Certification: Strongly Recommended, Not Required

The Certified Employer Program is the strongest version of employer engagement. It produces the best retention outcomes and the most coordinated career pathways.

However, it is strongly recommended, not required for Empower implementations. Smaller pilots or programs in regions with very limited employer options may begin without formal certification while still implementing the other three Non-Negotiables. The goal is to build toward certification as employer relationships develop.

Key Takeaway

The Certified Employer Program transforms job placement from a one-sided push into a two-way pipeline. Certification standards ensure employers understand ALICE realities, provide livable wages, and partner with the employer liaison on retention. Post-placement liaison and navigator support keeps employees stable. The result: career pathways, not revolving doors.

Employer certification is the retention engine. Without it, you're funding job placement into a revolving door. With it, you're funding career pathways backed by employers who stay connected long after the hire.
For Funders and Policymakers

Employer certification is the retention engine. Without it, you're funding job placement into a revolving door. With it, you're funding career pathways backed by employers who understand the transition, provide meaningful benefits, and stay connected to the employer liaison and navigator team long after the hire. That's the difference between a placement statistic and a family that stays above the poverty line.
