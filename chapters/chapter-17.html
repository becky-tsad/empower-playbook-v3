Getting Started

You've read the model. You've seen the evidence. Here's what happens next.

Executives Program Managers Funders 8 min read

You've read the model. You've seen the evidence. Here's what happens next.

If You're an Organization Ready to Implement

  1. Assess readiness. Use the Community Readiness Assessment (Chapter 13) to evaluate whether your infrastructure, partnerships, and political environment can support an Empower implementation. Be honest. Launching before you're ready produces the exact outcomes the model was built to replace.
  2. Contact The Poverty Solution. Implementation requires a TPS technical assistance partnership. This ensures methodology fidelity — the same standards that produced 926 families served and 83 at full self-sufficiency.
  3. Secure anchor funding. A 125-family pilot budgets at $1.0M–$1.1M for Year 1. Identify your anchor funder (government contract, major foundation, or combination) before committing to a launch timeline.
  4. Build your Phase 1 team. Hire the four core roles (Chapter 8). Begin training through the Empower Academy (Chapter 14). Map your services, recruit your first employers, and build your data infrastructure (Chapter 7).
  5. Launch Phase 2 only when Phase 1 is complete. Not before.

If You're a Funder Evaluating an Investment

What you're investing in: Not a program. Economic infrastructure for rural communities. A model that returns $4.40 for every $1 invested, validated by independent evaluation, with replication already underway.

What to ask the implementing organization:

  • Are all four Non-Negotiables present? (Chapter 4)
  • Is the navigator commitment 18+ months? (Chapter 4.2)
  • Does the team structure include all four core roles? (Chapter 8)
  • Is there a sustainability plan beyond your grant? (Chapter 10)
  • Can they pass the Quick Diagnostic? (Chapter 9)

How to position your investment: See Chapter 10.2 for audience-specific framing. Your board will want to know: What's the ROI? What's the evidence tier? What makes this different from other models? The answers are in Chapters 3 and 12.

If You're a Government Decision-Maker

The budget case: $2,052 per family in direct cost. $744K in annual TennCare savings. $183K in new payroll tax revenue. 26 families off SNAP. 20 off TennCare. Every family that reaches self-sufficiency reduces ongoing state expenditure permanently.

The contract structure: The Empower Method is designed for outcomes-based contracting. Define the performance benchmarks (families at 225% FPL, employment rate, benefits transitions completed), fund the implementation, and measure results. The data dashboard provides real-time visibility.

The political case: This model works in Speaker Cameron Sexton's district. It reduces government spending. It increases workforce participation. It generates payroll tax revenue. It's bipartisan by design — the data speaks to both "invest in people" and "reduce government dependency" audiences.

About the Organizations Behind This Model

Empower Upper Cumberland (EUC)

Backbone Organization: Upper Cumberland Human Resource Agency (UCHRA)

  • Structure: 501(c)(3) nonprofit
  • Annual Budget: $23M+
  • History: 50+ years serving the Upper Cumberland region
  • Service Area: 14 counties — Cannon, Clay, Cumberland, DeKalb, Fentress, Jackson, Macon, Overton, Pickett, Putnam, Smith, Van Buren, Warren, White
  • Population: ~330,000 residents
  • Infrastructure: Established relationships with employers, healthcare systems, childcare providers, educational institutions, and government agencies across the region

UCHRA isn't a startup. It's a half-century institution with the infrastructure, relationships, and operational capacity to execute systems-level change. Empower UC was built on that foundation — not alongside it.

Executive Director: Megan Spurgeon
Leads EUC's operations, external relationships, funding strategy, and policy advocacy. Drives the Leadership Team function described in Chapter 5.

Navigator Lead: Casey Dungan
Oversees the 17-navigator Economic Stability Team across 14 counties. Bridges ground-level family outcomes with program leadership.

Leadership Bench: EUC operates within UCHRA's broader organizational structure, providing institutional continuity, HR infrastructure, financial management, and governance oversight independent of any single leader. The model is designed to survive leadership transitions because it's embedded in an institution, not dependent on an individual.

The Poverty Solution (TPS)

Role: Technical assistance partner. Provides methodology design, training, quality assurance, and strategic guidance for Empower implementations.

Founder: Scott C. Miller — 40+ years designing poverty alleviation systems deployed in 100+ communities across the US and Canada. Author of Bootstraps and Benefits, Until It's Gone, and Transformational Leadership. Featured on CBS Evening News, CNN, NPR, PBS, Stanford Social Innovation Review.

COO: Becky Tsadilas — Partnerships, operations, and replication strategy.

TA Capacity: TPS currently supports EUC's flagship implementation and is building the technical assistance infrastructure for multi-site replication. The Beacon technology platform (benefits cliff calculation, AI decision support, compliance reporting) is designed for multi-tenant deployment — each implementation gets its own secure instance with state-specific benefit rules, reducing the marginal cost of each additional site.

Scalability of TA: TPS is developing a tiered partnership model:

  • Tier 1: Full technical assistance — methodology implementation, ongoing support, quality assurance
  • Tier 2: Training and certification — Empower Academy access, periodic check-ins, methodology audit
  • Tier 3: Self-service — playbook + Beacon platform access for organizations with existing implementation capacity

This tiered approach ensures that TPS's TA capacity scales with demand rather than constraining it.

TOPI Network

EUC operates within Tennessee's Opportunity Pilot Initiative (TOPI) network alongside peer organizations:

  • United Way of Greater Knoxville
  • East Tennessee Collective
  • STRONG Families

These organizations share learnings, develop collaborative funding strategies, and present a unified case for statewide expansion of the poverty exit model. The TOPI network gives funders and policymakers a multi-region evidence base, not a single-site anecdote.
