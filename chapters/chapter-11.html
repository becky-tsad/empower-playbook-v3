Adapting the Model

The Empower Method is a framework, not a franchise. The four Non-Negotiables stay constant. Everything else flexes.

Program Managers 8 min read

Remember Sarah from Chapter 2 — single mom, two kids, stuck at 32 hours a week at the grocery store, trapped by benefits cliffs she didn't know existed? Sarah represents one population. But the barriers that trap families look different depending on who you serve. The model adapts. The principles don't.

The Core Question

How do you adapt the Empower Method to different populations, geographies, and resource environments — without breaking the four Non-Negotiables that make it work?

11.1

Adaptation by Population

If Your Population Is... Start Here Adapt By... The Non-Negotiables Still Apply Because...
TANF families Stage 1 (Getting Started) Core model — this is what Empower was built for Benefits cliffs are the central barrier; wraparound coordination clears the path to livable-wage careers and keeps families stable
Recovery / Substance Abuse Stage 1, extended Longer foundation phase. Stabilization (sobriety, housing, mental health) before career planning. Recovery-specific partners integrated into wraparound network. Without economic mobility after stabilization, relapse rates climb. The navigator relationship is the bridge from recovery to career.
Workforce Development Stage 3 or 4 Heavier employer certification emphasis. Soft skills training aligned to regional jobs. Families may already be employed — focus on advancement, not placement. Even employed families face benefits cliffs. Without navigation, promotions trigger benefit loss and workers cycle back to low-wage positions.
Re-entry Stage 1, extended Barrier removal focus — background check navigation, employer education on hiring returning citizens, housing access. Returning citizens face compounded cliffs and systemic barriers. Wraparound coordination prevents the revolving door.
Disability Services Stage 1 or 2 Accommodation coordination, SSI/SSDI benefits planning (these cliffs are some of the steepest), assistive technology access. Benefits cliff navigation is even more critical — disability benefits have especially harsh cliff points.
Mixed ALICE Stage 1 Full model implementation. Segment participants by barrier type, not demographic. The four Non-Negotiables work because they address the system — not a specific population.
The Key Insight

The populations change. The barriers shift. But the underlying system failure is the same everywhere: fragmented services, punitive benefits cliffs, no one owning the outcome, and no data connecting the pieces. The four Non-Negotiables solve that system failure regardless of who you serve.

11.2

Adaptation by Geography

Rural communities are not interchangeable. Upper Cumberland's 14 counties have specific conditions. Your region will have different ones. The model adapts — but you need to know what you're adapting to.

Localization Checklist

  • Identify your top 3 barriers. In Upper Cumberland, they were childcare, transportation, and employer scarcity. Yours might be housing, broadband, or healthcare access. Your wraparound coordination network must be built around your specific barriers.
  • Map existing services. Every agency, every program, every funding stream touching your population. You can't eliminate duplication or fill gaps if you don't know what already exists.
  • Assess your employer landscape. How many employers in your region pay $14.50+/hour? Which sectors are growing? Where are the career pathways — not just jobs, but trajectories? This determines your employer certification strategy.
  • Set a locally appropriate self-sufficiency target. The ALICE threshold (225% FPL) works in Upper Cumberland. In higher-cost regions, it may not reach actual stability. Define "done" based on what it actually costs to live in your community.
  • Identify your navigator talent pool. Navigators need to be embedded in their communities. In thin labor markets, recruiting navigators is itself a challenge. Plan for it.
  • Map your political landscape. The Leadership Team's policy advocacy function requires relationships with state legislators, agency commissioners, and local officials. Know who they are before you need them.
11.3

What NOT to Change

These are the lines you don't cross. Organizations that modify these elements get different results — worse results.

Navigator Relationship Length: 18–24 Months Minimum

Empower's data is unambiguous. Relationships under 6 months produce negative outcomes. They don't just fail to help — they harm. There are no shortcuts to trust-based systems coordination.

Benefits cliff proactive planning. Every family gets a cliff analysis before any income change. Not after. Not "when we have time." Before.

Unified data tracking. One dashboard. One intake. One source of truth visible to all partners. The moment you allow agencies to maintain separate systems, coordination collapses.

Coordination Protocols

Regular meetings. Named contacts. Follow-through tracking. The moment coordination becomes optional, it stops happening.

The outcome standard. The ALICE threshold — or your locally calibrated equivalent. Not "employed." Not "enrolled in training." Not "completed referral." Self-sufficiency achieved and maintained.

11.4

The Adaptation Test

Before launching a modified version of the Empower Method, run this check:

Question If No →
Do all four Non-Negotiables remain intact? You're building something different. Call it something else.
Is the navigator commitment 18+ months? Shorten it and you'll produce the negative outcomes the research predicts.
Is there a dedicated navigator role (not split with case management)? You'll get better case management, not systems coordination.
Does wraparound coordination extend through employment and into career retention? You're doing job placement, not poverty exit.
Is there a unified data system? Partners will default to silos. Coordination will collapse.

If all five answers are yes, you're running the Empower Method. Adapt everything else freely.

For Funders and Policymakers

The Empower Method has been proven with TANF families across 14 rural counties. It's already expanding into workforce development and recovery populations. The four Non-Negotiables hold. The adaptations work. The only question is which community applies it next.

Key Takeaway

The Empower Method adapts to different populations, geographies, and resource environments. The populations change and the barriers shift, but the underlying system failure is the same everywhere. The four Non-Negotiables solve that system failure regardless of who you serve or where you operate.

The Empower Method is a framework, not a franchise. The four Non-Negotiables stay constant. Everything else flexes to fit your population, your geography, and your resources.
