Frequently Asked Questions

Answers for executives, program managers, and funders.

Executives Program Managers Funders 6 min read

For Executives

How much does implementation cost?

Direct cost per family is $2,052 in navigator support. Full program cost including infrastructure, training, data systems, and leadership is $8,200–$8,800 per family. A 125-family pilot budgets at $1.0M–$1.1M for Year 1. Costs decrease per family as you scale.

What's the minimum staff required?

Four core roles are non-negotiable: Executive Director, Program Director, Project Coordinator, Lead Navigator. Add navigators based on enrollment — caseloads must remain manageable. A 50-family pilot needs 4 core roles plus 1–2 navigators. See Chapter 8.

How long until we see results?

Phase 1 (foundation) takes 6 months with no families enrolled. First enrollments begin in Phase 2 (months 6–12). First families approaching the ALICE threshold in Phase 3 (months 12–24). Plan for 24 months minimum before reporting graduation outcomes.

What if we don't have all the partner agencies?

Start with what you have. The Collective Impact Team builds the network — it doesn't require a complete one on day one. But you do need a backbone organization and at least 3–5 committed partners before enrolling families. See Chapter 7, Phase 1 checklist.

Do we need The Poverty Solution's involvement?

Current model: TPS partnership is required for methodology fidelity and technical assistance. The playbook provides the framework; TPS provides the implementation support, training, and quality assurance.

For Program Managers

How do I train existing staff on the navigator model?

Through the Empower Academy training sequence (Chapter 14). Key distinction: navigators coordinate across systems; case managers refer to services. Staff with case management backgrounds can transition, but they must shift from referral thinking to coordination thinking. That requires training, not just a title change.

What if our data systems can't integrate?

Start with what's achievable. A shared spreadsheet is better than no shared data. A unified dashboard is the goal, but any system where partners see the same information in real time satisfies the Non-Negotiable. The Beacon platform is designed for this, but it's not the only option.

How do I handle staff who resist the change?

The shift from case management to navigation is significant. Some staff will embrace it. Others won't. The hiring guidance in Chapter 8 applies: problem solvers thrive in this model; checkbox people don't. Invest in training and give people time to adapt — but don't compromise the methodology to accommodate resistance.

What's the ideal caseload per navigator?

EUC operates with manageable caseloads designed to prevent families from getting lost. The exact number depends on geography (rural distance increases travel time), population complexity, and available wraparound resources. The principle: if a navigator can't maintain genuine relationship-based coordination with every family, the caseload is too high.

How do we handle families who aren't progressing?

Not every family will graduate above the ALICE threshold in 18–24 months. Some face barriers that take longer. The navigator commitment extends as needed. What matters is trajectory — is the family moving? If not, the navigator and Program Director assess whether additional wraparound resources, a different approach, or more time is the answer. Dropping a family because they're not hitting milestones fast enough reproduces the system failure the model is designed to solve.

For Funders

What evidence supports the model?

Randomized comparison by MEF Associates (Year 1) showed 11% higher positive outcome rate and $400/month income advantage vs. standard services. Urban Institute conducted the ROI analysis. Control group families voluntarily transitioned to the Empower model by Year 2 because outcomes were superior. See Chapter 3 for full evidence review.

How does this differ from other collective impact initiatives?

Most collective impact initiatives coordinate services. Empower integrates benefits cliff navigation — the single tool most poverty programs lack — into a three-team architecture that simultaneously serves families, coordinates systems, and advocates for policy change. The four Non-Negotiables make it a poverty exit system. See Chapter 12.3 for competitive positioning.

What's the cost per family served?

$2,052 in direct navigator support. $8,200–$8,800 fully loaded including infrastructure. Returns 4.4:1 in economic value. Compare to the ongoing taxpayer cost of maintaining a family on public assistance indefinitely.

How do you measure success?

Families reaching 225% of the Federal Poverty Level — the ALICE threshold where actual self-sufficiency begins. Not "enrolled in services." Not "attended workshops." Not "referred to agencies." Income above the threshold where a family can survive without public assistance. That's the only metric that matters.

How do you ensure the model is implemented with fidelity?

The Poverty Solution provides technical assistance, training, and quality assurance. The four Non-Negotiables are the fidelity test — if any one is absent, it's not the Empower Method. Chapter 11's Adaptation Test provides the specific checklist.
